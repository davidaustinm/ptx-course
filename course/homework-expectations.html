    Section Written homework expectations

    Grading and resubmissions.

    Each exercise will be graded as
    E (exemplary)
    🔗
    which is 4 out of 4 points,
    🔗
    S (Satisfactory)
    🔗
    which is 3 out of 4 points, or
    🔗
    NY (not yet)
    🔗
    which is 0 out of 4 points.
    🔗
    🔗
    If you earn “S” or “NY”, you may revise and resubmit that exercise according to the following parameters:
    1. Make sure you include the problem number.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Rewrite your entire solution as if you are doing it for the first time (unless I direct you otherwise). You need not restate the question in full.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Write at least two sentences about what you learned from re-doing the problem. For instance, you might write “I used to think that …, but now I understand that …”
      🔗
      🔗
    4. You must have submitted an honest attempt on the problem by the original deadline (or requested an extension) in order to resubmit. If you did not submit the exercise originally, see me to discuss options.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Each week, you may submit up to two revised exercises, within two weeks of their original due date.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Collaboration and Academic Honesty.

    You are allowed to collaborate on the homework since this is a good way to learn together.. However, you must be academically honest and adhere to the expectations for collaboration. A good self-test is this: am I using collaboration for greater understanding of course content? If yes, you are likely being academically honest. If, however, you are using collaboration to find answers that you don’t understand in order to gain “points”, you are likely being academically dishonest.
    🔗
    To help support you in being a student of honesty and integrity, here are our guidelines for collaboration on written homework:
    • “Collaboration” means “working together at roughly the same stage of the exercise”. It’s not a collaboration if one person does the work and then shares the completed product with another.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You may ask me at any time up to 24 hours before a homework set is due about the ideas in an exercise and even for hints, provided you have made an honest attempt at the exercise before asking me.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You must write your solutions in your own words, and the words you write need to make sense to you. You should be able to explain your thinking aloud to me if I were to ask you about it.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You may not collaborate with people outside our class on homework exercises: not other students, not tutors in the math center, not other professors.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You may not use internet resources other than our textbook and Sage: not Google generally, not Chegg, not ChatGPT, not other online “help” resources.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You may not copy from any other source (another student or the internet). Again: you can ask me for help and support. Please do this instead of resorting to any kind of academic dishonesty.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Overall Expectations.

    For each of these assignments, here are expectations for how you present your work.
    1. Use your best handwriting. Work in pencil. (Typeset work is certainly acceptable, but not required.)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Provide a careful statement of each problem. (Writing simply “#3” and then your answer is insufficient; it is not necessary to re-state every word, but you should at least provide a summary to indicate the context. Clearly state the exercise number prior to every problem statement: “Exercise #3: Consider each matrix below …”.)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. As noted above in the “writing mathematics” section, explanations are required: write in complete sentences, using correct spelling, punctuation, and grammar. The reasoning that leads to answers is at least as important as the answers that result. For example, you might write “Through row operations, we see that x = 3, y = -2, z = 7, and thus the original system has a unique solution.” Every homework exercise should have at least one complete sentence that either states what you plan to do or what you have found.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Whenever a picture or image may be helpful, include one. And label each appropriately; an image without labels lacks meaning.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Use correct mathematical language. We will place key emphasis on language throughout the course. For instance, from MTH 204 it would be valid to say “Thus, we have shown that the system of equations is inconsistent,” but it would be incorrect to say “Thus, we have shown that the matrix is inconsistent.” The term inconsistent applies to a system of equations, but not a matrix itself.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. For most problems, you should think of your work in two phases: (a) figure out what’s going on mathematically, which could involve crossing out work or backtracking, and (b) writing up your thinking carefully. Communication is extremely important so please think about separating these two tasks.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    These assignments are usually not the type that you can sit down and do all of the problems the day they are due. Rather, they will take careful thought well in advance. Working regularly and consistently over several days’ time will serve you well.
    🔗
    Your best work is expected on each homework assignment. Planning ahead and giving yourself an appropriate amount of time to work will enable you to meet these standards. Consider these assignments your opportunity to show me how well you understand recent material.
