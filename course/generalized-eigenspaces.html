    Worksheet Generalized Eigenspaces
    

    Suppose that \(T:V\to V\) is an operator with minimal polynomial \(p(x)=(x-\lambda)^k q(x)\) with \(q(\lambda)\neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} G_\lambda = \nul((T-\lambda I)^k) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    is called the generalized eigenspace. An element \(\vvec\) in \(G_\lambda\) is called a generalized eigenvector.
    🔗

    1.

    (a)

    Suppose that \(\vvec\) is a generalized eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\) Explain why \((T-\lambda I)^k\vvec = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    What can you conclude about the annihilating polynomial \(p_\vvec\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Suppose that \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) are two distinct eigenvalues. Use these observations to explain why \(G_\lambda \cap G_\mu = \{0\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    (a)

    Suppose that \(\dim V = 5\) and \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) are subspaces of \(V\) with \(\dim V_1 = 3\) and \(\dim V_2 = 2\text{.}\) Then there are bases \(\{\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\vvec_3\}\) for \(V_1\) and \(\{\vvec_4,\vvec_5\}\) for \(V_2\text{.}\) Explain why the set
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{\vvec_1,\vvec_2,\vvec_3,\vvec_4,\vvec_5\} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    is linearly dependent.
    🔗
    Use this fact to show that \(V_1 \cap V_2 \neq \{0\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    (a)

    Suppose that \(p(x)=(x-2)^3(x-4)^2\text{.}\) If \(\wvec\) is in \(\range((T-4I)^2)\text{,}\) explain why \((T-2I)^3(\wvec)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Explain why \(\range((T-4I)^2) \subset \nul((T-2I)^3)=G_2\) and hence \(\dim \range((T-4I)^2) \leq \dim G_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Explain why
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dim G_4 + \dim \range((T-4I)^2) = \dim V. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Explain why
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dim G_4 + \dim G_2 \geq \dim V. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(G_4 \cap G_2 = \{0\}\text{,}\) explain why \(\dim G_4 + \dim G_2 = \dim V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Explain why \(G_4 \oplus G_2 = V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Explain why both \(G_4\) and \(G_2\) are \(T\)-invariant.
    🔗
    If \(\bcal\) is a basis for \(G_4\) and \(\ccal\) is a basis for \(G_2\text{,}\) then \(\dcal=\bcal\cup\ccal\) is a basis for \(V\text{.}\) Explain why
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \coords{T}{\dcal,\dcal} = \begin{bmatrix} \coords{T}{\bcal,\bcal} \amp \zerovec \\ \zerovec \amp \coords{T}{\ccal,\ccal} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    (a)

    What you have found here is generally true: If the minimal polynomial
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = (x-\lambda_1)^{k_1}(x-\lambda_2)^{k_2}\cdots(x-\lambda_m)^{k_m}, \end{equation*}
    🔗
    then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V = G_{\lambda_1} \oplus G_{\lambda_2} \oplus \cdots \oplus G_{\lambda_m} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    and there is a basis for \(V\) such that the matrix representing \(T\) is block diagonal, where the diagonal blocks are the representations of \(T|_{G_{\lambda_j}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This worksheet develops the structure of generalized eigenspaces and shows how they decompose a vector space into invariant subspaces corresponding to the factors of the minimal polynomial.
