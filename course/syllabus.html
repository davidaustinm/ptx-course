    Section Course outline

    Students sometimes come into a math class with expectations based on their previous experiences and general attitudes in our society. Here are some important facts that inform the way our class will be structured.
    • Math is social.
      Math is social: Popular culture loves stories about a solitary genius who works for years in isolation. In fact, most professional mathematicians work closely with others and learn new ideas by talking with one another. This will be a feature of our class. One of your best resources will be your fellow classmates so I will help you get to know one another and learn to talk about mathematics. Not only will this help you learn math, but you’ll find it to be a lot more fun.
      🔗
      🔗
    • We learn by doing.
      I watched a lot of the NBA playoffs this year (Yay, Thunder!), but I still can’t make free throws. The best way to learn anything is to do it. This means that the majority of time in our class meetings will be devoted to students working through activities with their classmates (math is social, right?). This gives me a chance to get to know all of you as learners of math and to provide better feedback based on your understanding.
      🔗
      🔗
    • You belong in this class.
      Sometimes students can feel anxious in their math class. Have other students already had calculus? Does everyone remember everything from their high school math classes? Does everyone already know everyone else except me? These are pretty natural concerns but please know that I will do my best to get to know you and work with you with complete acceptance of your prior experiences.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Math makes sense.
      Math makes sense: Based on your previous math courses, you may think that the point of math is to get a "right" answer as fast as you can using some algorithm that no one really understands. Nope. Mathematics is a framework for making sense of our world, and our goal in this class is to develop your ability to think mathematically and to express your ideas clearly. A former student said, “Math is a lot easier when you think about what it means.”
      🔗
      🔗
    • I want you to succeed.
      The most satisfying part of my job is seeing my students grow as learners of mathematics and watching their confidence grow. I have high expectations of you in this course, but I will also provide you with the support that you need to reach those expectations. I am always interested in hearing about your experience, whether you are feeling frustrated or whether you want to share some recent success, and what kind of help you need from me.
      🔗
      🔗
    • What I expect of you.
      I’ve described some things above that I will provide you. Because learning is a partnership, I also expect some things from you because they will help you be successful.
      🔗
      Be open to new experiences and new people. Share freely with one another and accept the contributions of your classmates with gratitude.
      🔗
      Be willing to accept that you may not understand everything when you first see it but that I believe in your ability to work with me and your classmates to arrive at a deep understanding of mathematics.
      🔗
      Stay focused when working for this class. You may not pay monetarily to use social media, but you pay with your attention, which is one of your most valuable resources. Checking your phone regularly disrupts your concentration and significantly detracts from your learning. This is why the people in Silicon Valley who create this technology don’t let their kids use it. You may have a legitimate need to check your notifications during class, but you should otherwise be able to keep your phone in your pack for the 50 minutes that we meet.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Equity.

    We live in a world where not all groups of people are always treated fairly. Please know that I am fully committed to fairness and equity and that in all aspects of this course I will not tolerate language, ideas, or behaviors that are hostile to groups of people, such as racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, and xenophobia. In new situations, people are sometimes uncertain about what language and ideas could be hostile or offensive. If you feel this way, I am more than willing to discuss these issues with you privately. I will always treat these conversations confidentially, with respect, and without judgment.

    AI Policy.

    This one-word policy is simple: don’t. Please don’t use generative AI to complete any assignments or to check your work in this course. As full disclosure, I will tell you that I use AI as part of a research project to develop a large-language model that will translate math research papers into braille for blind readers. This task is very different from actually learning mathematics, which is your job in this class. You may think:
    • “I will be using AI in my career.” With the current state of AI, you won’t be using it to learn new ways of thinking, like we’re trying to do in our course. For one, it’s not particularly good at doing mathematics, and it won’t help you develop an understanding of mathematics.
      🔗
      🔗
    • “I only use it to check my work when I don’t feel totally confident.” I am part of a math education research study that investigates how students learn math from textbooks. Interviews with students clearly show how using AI causes students to become less confident over time since they come to trust what comes from AI more than they trust their own work. Learning will be challenging and facing challenges requires you to have the confidence that you can do it. Please don’t surrender the trust that you have in your own abilities.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    One research study, among others, is described here: Are We Really Willing to Become Dumber?
    🔗
    Or as ChatGPT said when asked to translate this policy into pirate: “The seas of AI be treacherous and will dull yer skills. Ye’ll learn by yer own hand, not by a talking parrot spitting answers.”

    Subsection About MTH 204

    MTH 204 is our first semester course in linear algebra. You may find that linear algebra is a little different than some of the other math courses you have taken up to this point, and I think you’re going to enjoy it a lot. Roughly speaking, we have three overarching goals. First, we’d like you to learn how to reason about important mathematical concepts and to express your reasoning clearly and precisely. Second, linear algebra is shaping our society in profound ways, and we’d like you to understand this and to see the powerful ways in which mathematics is applied in the world around us. Finally, the mathematician Carl Cowen said that “no significant use of linear algebra happens without a computer” so we’ll be developing our ability to deploy linear algebraic thinking to solve realistic problems using a computer.
    🔗

    Catalog Description​ of MTH 204, Linear Algebra I.

    An introduction to systems of linear equations emphasizing conceptual understanding and computational thinking. Primary topics include solutions to linear systems, vector and matrix algebra, span and linear independence, bases, eigenvectors, and eigenvalues. Applications will be integrated throughout the course. Offered fall and winter semesters. Prerequisites: MTH 122 and MTH 123; or MTH 124; or assignment through Grand Valley math placement.”
    🔗
    🔗

    Course Content.

    We will study all or nearly all of the material in the first four chapters of our textbook, Understanding Linear Algebra. Your goal should be, as part of our ongoing work together, to read the text itself and ask questions as needed. Preview Activities will normally be assigned for credit (based on effort and completeness) as part of your Daily Prep Assignments; the other activities in the text will be a central focus of our in-class discussions and work, and it’s your responsibility (with lots of support from your instructor and one another) to make sure you understand them and work through them fully.
    🔗
    🔗

    Prerequisites.

    ​ MTH 122/123 or MTH 124 or GV placement. As you’ll see, this course doesn’t rely on prerequisites as much as your calculus class would, but there are some times when you’ll need to have a working knowledge of important concepts from algebra and trigonometry. I’m willing to help you if you feel rusty with a few of these ideas, but you are responsible for making sure you have a good understanding of the prerequisite knowledge
    🔗
    🔗

    Learning Objectives.

    After successful completion of this course, you will be able to:
    • Analyze systems of linear equations and describe their solution spaces.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Describe how the pivot positions of an augmented matrix influence the nature of the solution space to a linear system.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Demonstrate algebraic and geometric fluency with vectors.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Interpret questions about linear systems as questions about linear combinations of vectors.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Describe the concept of span of a set of vectors and apply it to determine the span of a set of vectors.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Describe the concept of linear independence of a set of vectors and apply it to determine whether a set of vectors is linearly independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Interpret matrix transformations as functions defined through matrix multiplication and create specific matrix transformations to perform geometric operations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate matrix sums, products, and multiples by scalar, and recognize matrix products as arising from composing matrix transformations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Recognize a basis for R n and use the coordinate system it defines to solve problems.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Recognize an invertible matrix and find its inverse.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Apply one of several algorithms for finding the determinant of a matrix, and use the determinant to determine if a matrix is invertible.
      🔗
      🔗
    • State the definition and intuitive meaning of an eigenvalue-eigenvector pair of a square matrix.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Apply a conceptual understanding of linear systems to find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a square matrix.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Demonstrate how the theory of eigenvalues and eigenvectors is used to solve problems in dynamical systems.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Use computer algebra technology as a tool to solve important problems in linear algebra.
      🔗
      🔗

    Subsection Proficiency quizzes

    This course has a set of learning targets that are intended to focus your attention on specific skills you should acquire to develop a deep understanding of linear algebra. Here are some things to know about why this is an important part of the course.
    • Learning is a process. You will not be penalized if you don’t demonstrate proficiency in a skill the first time, but you are expected to do so eventually.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Frequent assessment is good for learning. Think of these quizzes as a tool for learning rather than just assessment---studies show that retrieval of information makes it easier to recall that information later. This is called “the testing effect.’’
      🔗
      🔗
    • Keeping up with course material is important. These quizzes will be one way of informing both you and me about your progress in the course.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Knowing the basics well is crucial for tackling more in-depth problems. These targets were chosen because they are fundamental so you should think of them as building blocks. You will have time to practice these skills before, during, and after class, but we will also spend class time using them to tackle substantial problems.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Here are the nuts and bolts of how it works.
    • Each Thursday, we will have an in-class quiz that assesses some of these skills, which will be announced in advance.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Each skill will be graded on a four-point scale: “E’’ for “Exemplary’’ is four points, “S” for “Satisfactory is three points, and “NY’’ for “not yet’’ is zero points To earn an “E,’’ your solution needs to show all work and explanations, use correct notation, and arrive at a correct answer. In short, your work should be close to perfect.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If you did not successfully complete a learning target, you can try again next week. I won’t provide a lot of feedback because it’s very helpful for you to learn to assess your work independently of me. I am happy to talk with you about any of your work on the quizzes though.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Since you have multiple attempts on each target, there will be no makeup quizzes.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Each target will appear on two quizzes.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If you still haven’t successfully completed a target after your second attempt, you are welcome to talk with me about it during office hours (or another agreed upon time) within one week of the second attempt, and you can have another attempt.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Submitting work

    Submitting graded work.

    Make sure that your written homework is prepared neatly and well organized so that it’s easy to follow. There should be at least two phases to preparing your homework assignments: (1) solving the assigned problems and (2) writing up your solutions and editing. Be sure to keep them separate. If you are crossing out portions of your work or including arrows pointing to your next steps, you are combining the two phases and need to start the writing phase again once you’ve got a better understanding of the problem. Please include the problems in the order in which they are assigned. Make sure your name is clearly indicated.
    🔗
    Through our work in class and other things like written homework, we will be building from the ground up an understanding of linear algebra. In preparing your homework, you should only use results that we have developed in our class rather than something that shows up in an internet search that may be technically correct but not consistent with the framework that we have developed.
    🔗
    🔗

    Late work.

    While assignments will always have due dates, I recognize that life happens and that you don’t owe me an explanation when it does. Throughout the semester, you are allowed to submit an assignment late on three occasions provided you write to me beforehand and let me know when you’ll get it to me. Please note that this policy does not apply to learning target quizzes. If you are habitually having a hard time handing things in on time, please come and talk with me.

        🔗

It is more important than ever to be able to solve non-routine problems and be confident in attacking new ideas. If you work to make sense of ideas, you will be able to think creatively in new ways. If you can communicate these ideas to others, you will be able to really demonstrate that understanding. These skills are far more valuable to your future than any particular content you can learn in MTH 204. As a former student said: “linear algebra just makes sense!” (Alexis - the student who said that - now holds a PhD in mathematics and works in data science and machine learning.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Work hard. This is a 3-credit course, which carries an expectation of 6-9 hours of work each week outside of class. You will find that things come more easily if you work a little bit every day rather than planning a long study session before a due date.

Instagram, SnapChat, TikTok and other apps know this, and they were designed (designed by engineers and psychologists) to be addictive and persuasive. If you have any of these apps open during class or when you are committed to studying, you are guaranteed to be distracted. I strongly encourage you to dedicate time free from social media distractions so that you can think as clearly as possible about the ideas in our course.

      🔗
    • Seek help. You have three main resources for support with mathematical ideas: (1) office hours or an individual appointment with me, (2) your peers in class, and (3) the GVSU Math Center. Be honest with yourself and ask, “Do I really understand this?” If not, find someone who does and discuss the ideas with them.

    Subsection Academic Integrity

    Throughout this course, I hope that you learn and share ideas with your classmates. Please be aware of the following university policies regarding academic honesty.
    🔗

    Integrity of Scholarship and Grades.

    Truth and honesty: The principles of truth and honesty are recognized as fundamental to a community of teachers and scholars. The university expects that both faculty members and students will honor these principles and in so doing protect the validity of university grades. This means that all academic work will be done by the student to whom it is assigned without unauthorized aid of any kind. Instructors, for their part, will exercise care in the planning and supervision of academic work, so that honest effort will be positively encouraged. Compliance shall include compliance with the following specific rules:
    1. No student shall knowingly, without authorization, procure, provide, or accept any materials which contain questions or answers to any examination or assignment.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. No student shall, without authorization, complete, in part or in total, any examination or assignment for another person.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. No student shall, without authorization, allow any examination or assignment to be completed, in part or in total, by another person.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. No student shall knowingly plagiarize or copy the work of another person and submit it as his or her own.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. No student shall submit work that has been previously graded or is being submitted concurrently to more than one course without authorization from the instructor(s) of the class(es) to which the student wishes to submit it.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Plagiarism.

    Any ideas or material taken from another source for either written or oral presentation must be fully acknowledged. Offering the work of someone else as one’s own is plagiarism. The language or ideas taken from another may range from isolated formulas, sentences, or paragraphs to entire articles copied from books, periodicals, speeches, or the writing of other students. The offering of materials assembled or collected by others in the form of projects or collections without acknowledgment also is considered plagiarism. Any student who fails to give credit in written or oral work for the ideas or materials that have been taken from another is guilty of plagiarism.
